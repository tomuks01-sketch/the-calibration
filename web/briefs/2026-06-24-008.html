Crowd Signal #008 · 24 June 2026 · 3 min read

Thirteen calls in, a dead heat

The prediction-market ledger has now scored its first 13 calls. Our model's Brier score is 0.159 against the crowd's 0.162 — a statistical dead heat: ahead on six, behind on seven. After weeks of an empty scoreboard, this is the first real read, and we are publishing it — tie, selection caveat and all — exactly as it stands.

1 · What went on the record

Each call was locked the moment it was made: our probability written down next to the crowd's price at that instant, and left untouched. A market settles on its own timeline; thirteen of them have now resolved YES or NO, and each is scored with a Brier score — the squared distance from what was said to what happened, lower being better — for both our number and the crowd's. One caveat we state plainly: these are snapshot-scoped. We only log a call for markets that appear in the most-liquid snapshot at the time, so this is not a random sample of all of Polymarket — read the figures with that selection in mind.

2 · A dead heat

Across the thirteen, our model scored 0.159 and the crowd 0.162 — a gap of 0.003, which at this sample size is nothing. We were closer on six calls, the crowd on seven. That is exactly what a dead heat looks like, and it is the honest headline: no edge was claimed, and none has yet been found. Beating a liquid market's own price is supposed to be hard; thirteen calls in, we have not — and we are not going to dress up a coin's width of difference as a win.

3 · Where we were right, where we were wrong

The record cuts both ways, so here are both edges of it. Our best call was doubting the San Antonio Spurs to win the 2026 NBA title: the crowd hedged, our model was firmly low, and they didn't — Brier 0.22 for us versus 0.40 for the crowd. Our worst was Alexander Zverev at the French Open: we rated him a clear underdog, the crowd gave him more, and he won — Brier 0.57 for us against 0.43. We keep both on the page, the loss as loudly as the win.

4 · Three records now, not one

The prediction-market ledger is the slow one — markets resolve when they resolve. The other two move faster and are further along. The crypto forecasts have 126 resolved windows: a coin-flip on direction (as #007 reported) and a well-calibrated volatility band. The football model has 15 international matches scored, its most-likely outcome right two-thirds of the time, with a head-to-head against the bookmaker's odds about to cross the threshold where we can read it. Each record is public and graded the same way: win or lose, it stays.

5 · What this does — and doesn't — mean

Thirteen is an early read, not a verdict — the scoreboard still says low confidence, and it means it. A real edge over a liquid crowd, if one exists, would take many more resolved calls to separate from noise, and the snapshot-scoping is a genuine limit on what these numbers can claim. We are publishing now, at a dead heat, because the entire point is to be scored in public before we know the answer, not after. Every figure here lives on the Scoreboard, moving as the record grows.

Analytics only. These are transparent, documented probabilities scored in public against the crowd's own price — not predictions to act on, not betting, financial or investment advice, and no profit claim. A dead heat is not an edge; a single good or bad call proves nothing. Figures are snapshot-scoped and come from the live record, which moves continuously. Think for yourself.
