Crowd Signal #009 · 24 June 2026 · 3 min read

The bookmaker is sharper

Enough international matches have now resolved with odds attached to read our football model against the bookmaker's own price. Over 30 matches our RPS is 0.155; the bookmaker's is 0.138. Lower is better, so we are behind. This is the answer to the question the whole football model was built to ask, and the answer — for now — is that the market is sharper.

1 · The verdict

Each forecast is locked before kickoff next to the bookmaker's implied probabilities, and both are scored with RPS — the standard measure for a three-way win/draw/win call — the moment the match ends. Across the thirty graded so far, we were closer than the bookmaker on only 37% of them. Our most-likely outcome came in 64% of the time, which is fine, but "fine" is not "sharper than a market that prices injuries, line-ups and the morning's news." Our Elo runs on 49,000 historical results and sees none of that. The gap is small — 0.017 — but it is real and it is the wrong way round.

2 · Why a loss is the system working

We did not build this to beat the bookmaker on day one. We built it to be scored against one, in public, win or lose — so that any future claim of skill has a receipt. Most sites that sell football "AI" never show this number because it would end the sale. Ours is the headline: over thirty matches, the market beats us. If that never changes, the scoreboard will keep saying it.

3 · The first thing we changed

A measured gap is a target, so we made one documented change. Our scoreline model treated each team's goals as independent — a standard simplification that is known to under-price draws and the tight 0-0 and 1-1 results. We added the Dixon-Coles correction (from the 1997 paper that more or less founded modern football modelling), which lifts exactly those low-score cells. It is a documented constant, not a number we fitted to our thirty games — the same honesty rule as every other prior in the model. On a close fixture it nudges the draw probability up by about three points. It applies to new forecasts only; the matches already scored keep their old numbers.

4 · What we are not promising

Dixon-Coles is a refinement, not a magic wand. It is unlikely to fully close a gap to a market that knows who is injured and we don't, and it is entirely possible that with public, keyless data we simply never beat the bookmaker. We are not promising that we will. We are promising that the next thirty matches are scored the same way, that the correction either narrows the gap or it doesn't, and that the number is on the football page either way.

5 · Elsewhere on the record

The pattern holds across the board. Our crypto direction calls, now 154 resolved, remain a coin flip — no edge, as #007 said — while the volatility band stays calibrated. The prediction-market ledger is a dead heat with the crowd (#008). Three records, one rule: we publish the score we got, not the one we wanted.

Analytics only. These are transparent, documented probabilities scored in public against a bookmaker's implied odds — not predictions to act on, not betting, financial or investment advice, and no profit claim. Losing to the market is not a tip to bet the market; a model change is not a promise of improvement. Figures come from the live record and move continuously. Think for yourself.
